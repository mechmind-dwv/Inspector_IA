# Inspector IA — Ethics Addendum

> This document is a **non-binding social contract**. It is intentionally kept
> separate from `LICENSE` so the code remains compatible with the standard MIT
> License and the OSI definition of open source. We ask — but do not legally
> compel — every user of Inspector IA to honour the following.

## 1. Purpose

Inspector IA is built **only** to assist investigative journalism in the public
interest. If your intended use does not serve the public interest, please do
not use this software.

## 2. Mandatory human oversight

Inspector IA produces **risk alerts**, not findings. Every alert must be
reviewed and corroborated by a qualified human journalist (or analogous
professional) before any publication, accusation, or operational action.

## 3. Red lines

We will publicly disassociate the project from any actor using Inspector IA
for:

- Mass surveillance of private citizens.
- Discrimination based on protected characteristics (race, religion, gender,
  sexual orientation, disability, etc.).
- Automated publication, denouncement, or sanction without human review.
- Sale of data or derived intelligence to third parties.
- Political targeting, harassment, or manipulation campaigns.
- Any use that puts journalistic sources at risk.

## 4. Transparency expectations

We ask users to:

1. Disclose, in any publication that relies on Inspector IA outputs, that the
   tool was used as **one** input among others.
2. Preserve the audit trail of the IRA computation that supported any
   published claim, for at least 2 years.
3. Report serious abuse or misuse to `ia.mechmind@gmail.com`.

## 5. Acknowledgement of AI tooling used

Inspector IA itself is being built with assistance from several AI systems
(at audit time: DeepSeek, Gemini and Claude were used for code generation,
architecture review and documentation drafting). These tools are **not**
co-authors and are **not** team members; they are development aids whose
output is reviewed by the human maintainer.

## 6. Why a non-binding addendum and not a custom license?

A license with use-restrictions would:

- Make the project **non-OSI-compliant**, hurting adoption by responsible
  organisations whose policies require OSI-approved licences.
- Be **legally weak in many jurisdictions** anyway.
- Risk creating a false sense of safety: real safeguards live in community
  norms, peer review and operational controls — not in a clause no court will
  enforce.

We prefer **moral clarity + legal clarity**, kept in separate documents.
